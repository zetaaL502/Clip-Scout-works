Dubai’s entire business model is built on one word: "Untouchable."
For 30 years, they’ve sold a dream to the world's richest people: Move your money here, and the chaos of the Middle East will never cross our border. No income tax, zero crime, and no politics.
And it worked beyond anyone’s wildest expectations. Dubai’s millionaire population has doubled since 2014 to more than 81,000 people. Last year alone, 10,000 millionaires moved in—more than London, Singapore, and Monaco combined. Dubai wasn’t just winning the race for global wealth; it was absorbing it.
But in one single weekend—everything changed. And what happened next revealed a cold, hard truth about what it actually means to be ultra-wealthy in a crisis.
Let me take you back to Saturday, March 1st 2026.
You are sitting in your penthouse on the Palm Jumeirah. One of the most expensive addresses on the planet. You have built your entire life here. Your business is registered here. Your kids go to one of the best international schools in the world. You moved to Dubai because it was the one place in the entire Middle East where none of the chaos could touch you.
Then you hear it. A sound you have never heard before in this city. Explosions. Not fireworks. Not construction. Actual explosions shaking your windows.
You look outside and you see smoke rising from the direction of the Fairmont Palm hotel — one of the most iconic luxury hotels in the world — just a few kilometres from your home. Your phone starts blowing up. Your wife is asking what is happening. Your kids are scared. And in that moment you make a decision. It is time to go.
This is not a hypothetical story. This is what thousands of Dubai's wealthiest residents experienced that weekend. And what happened next revealed something extraordinary about money, power, safety, and what it truly means to be ultra wealthy in 2026.
So let me give you the full context first. Because this story does not make sense without understanding how Dubai got here.
Dubai spent the last 30 years positioning itself as the Switzerland of the Middle East. A neutral, safe, business friendly zone where the politics of the region stopped at the border. No income tax. No capital gains tax. World class infrastructure. And critically — safety. Absolute. Guaranteed. Undisputed safety.
And it worked beyond anyone's wildest expectations.
An estimated 9,800 millionaires moved to Dubai in 2025 alone, bringing 63 billion dollars in wealth with them. The city became home to 237 centimillionaires — people worth 100 million dollars or more — and at least 20 billionaires. These are not tourists passing through. These are people who moved their entire lives, their families, their businesses, their assets to Dubai because they believed in the promise.
And the promise was simple. Come here. Build here. Your money is safe. Your family is safe. The region around us might be complicated but inside these borders everything is fine. That promise held for three decades. And then in one weekend it was broken.
On the night of March 1st 2026, Iran launched a series of missile and drone strikes targeting the UAE. The strikes hit Dubai International Airport — one of the busiest airports on the planet, handling over 90 million passengers a year. Debris struck the area around the Burj Al Arab. The Fairmont Palm hotel caught fire, the flames captured in real time by dozens of influencers who posted it to their millions of followers within minutes.
Within hours Dubai International Airport was closed. All commercial flights grounded. UAE airspace was restricted. And over a million passengers found themselves stranded with absolutely no way out.
For ordinary people this was a nightmare. Sleeping on airport floors. Refreshing booking apps every few minutes. Watching their connecting flights disappear one by one. Calling airlines that simply were not answering.
But for the ultra wealthy it was a completely different experience. Because when you have serious money you do not wait in queues. You do not sleep on airport floors. You pick up the phone, you call your people, and you solve the problem. No matter what it costs.
But there was a hidden wall that even money couldn't easily climb: War Risk Insurance.
Most people don't realize that every aircraft is governed by specific insurance clauses. The second a missile strike is confirmed, standard aviation insurance is voided. For a private jet owner, taking off meant risking a 60-million-dollar asset with zero coverage. This created a massive supply shortage. Most operators simply refused to fly. The few who were willing to risk it suddenly held the most valuable seats on earth.
And this is where the story gets truly insane.
Private jet brokers reported that demand completely overwhelmed supply overnight. Ameerh Naran, CEO of Vimana Private Jets, said his company received more than 100 client inquiries in a single night. One hundred people. All with the same message. Get me out of Dubai right now. I do not care what it costs.
Flights from Riyadh to Europe were being quoted at up to 350,000 dollars. Let that number sit with you for a moment. Three hundred and fifty thousand dollars. For a single one way flight. That is more than most people earn in five years. And wealthy residents were paying it without hesitation.
A small private jet from the region to Istanbul — normally priced around 28,000 dollars — was being sold for 85,000 euros. Three times the normal rate. Still sold out immediately. One family drove six hours through the desert to reach Oman, then paid 200,000 dollars to charter a flight from Muscat to Geneva, all while the situation in Dubai was still developing. Think about what that requires. The calm. The network. The resources. The ability to look at a 200,000 dollar bill and say yes, that works, let us go.
The private jet industry had its biggest single weekend in years. And every single dollar of it came from people who were supposed to be living in the safest city in the Middle East.
But here is the twist that almost nobody picked up on. And honestly this is my favourite part of the whole story because it tells you everything about how the ultra wealthy think differently from everyone else.
Some of Dubai's wealthy residents were not just trying to leave. Some were desperately trying to come BACK. Chartering private jets. Paying enormous sums. Not to escape — but to return to Dubai. In the middle of the crisis.
Why? Tax.
Dubai's zero income tax only applies if you spend enough days in the UAE each year to qualify as a tax resident. This is often referred to as the 183-day rule, though it varies by country. If you leave and stay away too long you lose your residency status. And the moment you lose that status your home country's tax authority — whether that is the UK, Germany, France, Australia, wherever — can claim you as a tax resident again and come after you for potentially millions in unpaid taxes.
Imagine you are a billionaire who saved 40 million dollars in taxes by living in Dubai. If you flee to London and stay there too long, the UK government can reclaim you. Suddenly, you owe them 45% of your global income.
So you had this completely surreal situation playing out in real time. Missiles in the air. Hotels on fire. A city in crisis. And wealthy expats on the phone to their accountants calculating exactly how many days they could afford to stay away before their tax arrangements collapsed. Some were paying 350,000 dollars to leave. Others were paying just as much to come back. All based on spreadsheets. All based on calculations. Because at that level of wealth the tax saving from one year of Dubai residency can be worth tens of millions of dollars. The math still worked even with 350,000 dollar flights.
Money does not panic. Money calculates.
Now I want to give you my honest take on all of this. Because I think there are several things happening here that the mainstream coverage is completely missing.
First — this story exposes something that makes a lot of people uncomfortable but needs to be said. While the ultra wealthy were paying hundreds of thousands of dollars to fly out on private jets and chartering SUV convoys through the desert, hundreds of thousands of ordinary people were left with nothing. Migrant workers. Tourists on package holidays. Families who spent months saving for their trip. People without connections, without resources, without options.
Just waiting. Hoping. Refreshing the same booking app over and over again hoping a seat would appear. The same city that built its entire brand on luxury and aspiration revealed in one weekend exactly how unequal that aspiration really is. Two completely different cities existing in the same place at the same time. One city where a crisis costs you 350,000 dollars. Another city where a crisis leaves you sleeping on an airport floor.
Second — the wealthy people leaving Dubai are not losing. They are not giving up on the dream of low taxes. They are just updating their spreadsheets and shopping for a new location. Istanbul is seeing a surge in wealthy arrivals right now. Singapore is positioning itself as the obvious alternative for serious money looking for a stable Asian hub. Riyadh is accelerating its Vision 2030 plans with renewed urgency.
The wealth does not disappear in a crisis. It moves. And wherever it moves next is going to change dramatically in the years ahead.
So what actually happens to Dubai now? What is the realistic outcome here?
Short term — the next six to twelve months — Dubai will struggle. Real estate prices that were already showing signs of overheating will face serious pressure. UBS ranked Dubai as having the fifth highest real estate bubble risk of any major city in the world. The conflict has accelerated a correction that was probably coming anyway. Business investment will slow. Some of that business will come back. Some of it will not.
Medium term — if a ceasefire holds — Dubai has everything it needs to recover. The infrastructure is world class. The legal framework is business friendly. None of those things changed.
Long term — I genuinely believe Dubai comes back. It always has. But the city that emerges from this will be different. More cautious. Possibly more humble about the promise of absolute safety that it can no longer guarantee. Many residents who stayed described life continuing largely as normal. The Dubai World Cup horse racing event went ahead right in the middle of everything. Horses from 16 countries still competed. The show went on.
That is Dubai's mentality. The show always goes on. But the ultra wealthy who fled on 350,000 dollar private jets will make their calculations. Some will come back. Some will not. But the ones who do come back will do so with their eyes open.
